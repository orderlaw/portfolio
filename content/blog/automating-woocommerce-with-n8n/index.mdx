A UK e-commerce brand was processing a few hundred orders a week. Their fulfilment team was spending four hours a day doing things that should have taken zero — manually updating order statuses, emailing dispatch notes, logging shipments into a tracking sheet.

They asked me to fix it. Here's what I built, and what the process actually looked like.

---

## The situation before

Every order followed the same manual path:

1. Order comes in via WooCommerce
2. Someone checks it, assigns it to a warehouse pick list
3. Pick gets done, warehouse emails a dispatch note
4. Someone updates the WooCommerce order status
5. Customer gets a shipping email — sometimes hours later

The problem wasn't that any single step was hard. It's that the chain had five human hand-offs, and each one was a chance to miss something. At volume, some did miss. Every day.

**The actual cost wasn't the time — it was the trust erosion.** Customers emailed support about orders that had already shipped. The support team couldn't answer quickly because the tracking sheet was always a few hours behind.

---

## What I built

n8n sits in the middle of the entire fulfilment loop. It listens to WooCommerce webhooks, makes decisions, and writes back — without anyone watching.

### Order intake

A webhook fires on every new WooCommerce order. n8n validates the payload, checks stock availability against the warehouse system, and routes the order to the correct pick queue. Orders with special handling flags (fragile, oversized, combined shipments) branch off into a separate validation flow before being queued.

No human decides which queue an order goes to. That decision is encoded.

### Status updates

When the warehouse marks a pick as complete, their system sends a webhook. n8n catches it, maps the status to WooCommerce's schema, and updates the order — automatically. The dispatch email goes to the customer within seconds of the pick being confirmed, not when someone gets around to it.

### Error routing

Payment failures, address validation errors, and out-of-stock situations each have their own branch. Instead of silently failing or creating support tickets, they route to a dedicated Slack channel with context. The team sees the problem before the customer emails.

### The tracking sheet

The manually-maintained spreadsheet got replaced by a Supabase table. n8n writes to it on every state change. The ops director now has a live view instead of a document that's always two hours stale.

---

## What I'd do differently

**Start with error paths, not the happy path.** I built the core happy-path flow first, which made sense for getting something working quickly — but the error branches took almost as long to build and were more important in practice. Most of the real-world edge cases live in the errors.

**Don't map statuses one-to-one.** WooCommerce has its status model, the warehouse system has its own. I initially tried to keep them in sync directly. That broke when the warehouse added a new status mid-project. Building a translation layer that maps between them independently is more work upfront and much more resilient.

**Log everything, even when you think you won't need it.** n8n's built-in execution history helps, but adding explicit logging to Supabase for every workflow run meant the client could see what happened on any order without me needing to dig through execution logs.

---

## Results

Four hours of daily manual processing gone. Dispatch emails now go out automatically, within seconds of pick completion. Support tickets related to order status dropped around 70% in the first month. No missed orders since go-live.

The workflow runs. Nobody watches it.

> "We went from chasing orders to trusting them."
